WIND4D: Development of a high-fidelity massively parallel Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) solver from scratch, Ph.D.

Description: I developed the WIND4D solver to model and solve any fluid problems. The code is written in C and MPI from scratch, approximately has more than 30000 lines of code. A concurrent precursor method (CPM) was implemented in the solver. The Finite Volume Method (FVM) was used to discretize the governing equations. The solver has both Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) and Large Eddy Simulation (LES) capabilities. There are several LES turbulence models (Lagrangian-averaged scale-dependent (LASD), Lagrangian-averaged scale-independent (LASI), Planar-averaged scale-dependent (PASD), Dynamic model of Germano, and Smagorinsky model, and many more) to parametrize the subgrid-scale (SGS) terms. A sophisticated wall model based on local similarity law was implemented to calculate the shear stresses at the surface. HYPRE multigrid solver was used to solve the pressure Poisson equation. The code has a Time-Adaptive Wind-Turbine Model with Yaw Implementation. The code also features the shifted periodic boundary conditions (SPBC), two methods were implemented to achieve this. The code ran on 400 cores (that's the user limit, I believe the code can run on thousands of cores) in the Center for Advanced Computing and Research (CARC) at the University of New Mexico (UNM). The code provides other important features too. Several publications were published using the solver.

Topic: Analysis of Wind Farms under Different Yaw Angles and Wind Speeds, Energies Journal 2023

Project Description: A computational investigation using a three-by-three wind turbine array was conducted, employing large eddy simulation (LES) to evaluate wind farm performance. Nine LEScases were considered, incorporating three wind speeds (7.3 ms−1, 10.4 ms−1, and 4.3 ms−1) and three yaw angles (30◦, 20◦, and 0◦), with nearly constant turbulence intensity (TI) at 12.0%. The impact of wind speed and yaw angles on wake characteristics and power outputs were analyzed.

Findings: Wind speed variations have little influence in wake characteristics for any yaw angle, except for the wind speed 4.3 ms−1 at the yaw angle of 20°. The wake deflection is higher at higher wind speeds and with yawed wind turbines. However, the degree of wake steering is reduced at lower wind speeds, resulting in increased blockage losses. At lower wind speeds, the turbulence intensity profiles show reduced deflection, particularly at the yaw angle of 20 degrees when the wind speed is 4.3 ms−1. As the flow progresses downstream, the difference in the degree of deflection between the profiles becomes more apparent, regardless of the yaw angle, due to the varying wind speeds. This wake behavior significantly affects the power generated, especially when the turbines are yawed. The power losses are higher for the lower wind speeds, especially for the yaw angle of 20° at the wind speed of 4.3 ms−1.

Title: Numerical Perspectives on Wind Turbine Wakes, the American Physical Society Division of Fluid Dynamics (APS-DFD) Annual Meeting 2019

Project Description: The influences of gap between turbine and ground were investigated. The ceneter of the turbine is located at 4.0D downstream from the inflow plane and at the middle in the spanwise direction. The dimensions of the test domain in terms rotor diameter are Lx ≈ 16.0D, Ly ≈ 8.0D and Lz ≈ 16.0D. Rotor diameter, D = 126m for both cases. case1: gap 0.25D(27m) while hub height = 90m. case2: gap 0.35D(40.5m) while hub height = 103.5m.

Findings: Higher momentum flux through the gap for 0.35D case compared to 0.25D case. The wake for 0.25D case persists longer distance compared to 0.35D case. Velocity gets more accelerated through the gap for the 0.35D case compared to the 0.25D case. Velocity gets more accelerated even outside the rotor plane for the 0.35D case compared to the 0.25D case. Higher turbulence intensity(TI) for the 0.35D case compared to the 0.25D case. Higher TI suggests that the wake is recovered faster. Higher turbulence intensity(TI) even with lower incoming turbulence intensity for the 0.35D case compared to the 0.25D case.

Title:Heat and Mass Transfer in Axisymmetric Pipe Flow

Project Description: The Navier-Stokes equations along with heat and mass transfer equations for this present problem are solved with finite volume method (FVM) with second-order accurate discretized equations in cylindical coordinates. All the values for dimensionalized again with the physical characteristic scale values. The Reynolds number, Re = 10425.25, Prandtl number, Pr = 0.017 and Schmidt number, Sc = 76.48; Cw = 0.14 and Cb = 0.03. Comparison of mass transfer coefficient for numerical case against empirical cases were perfomed.

Findings: Thermal boundary layer develops only near the inflow region, then it stays steady because of low heat transfer (due to low Biot number number) in the radial direction. Concentration boundary layer also has develops near the inlet which has sudden peak at this region because of significant temperature and velocity changes. The empirical formulas used to calculate the mass transfer coefficients are applicable to turbulent flows, where the present case it’s laminar, which justifies the differences between the coefficients.
